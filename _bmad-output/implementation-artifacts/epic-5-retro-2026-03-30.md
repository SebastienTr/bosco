## Successes

1. **Infrastructure, policy, and product work landed as one coherent hardening pass**  
   Epic 5 did not just add backend plumbing; it connected operations, legal surfaces, schema changes, and user-facing compliance flows into one production-readiness layer.

2. **Architecture discipline remained strong under privileged/security-sensitive work**  
   Service-role access stayed isolated behind Tier 1/Tier 2 boundaries, logging was standardized, and account deletion eventually aligned with the containment rules instead of leaking admin concerns into route code.

3. **Observability became a reusable platform capability instead of one-off instrumentation**  
   The `withLogging(...)` wrapper, structured action logs, Sentry integration, and `/api/health` endpoint are reusable foundations that future epics can build on rather than reinvent.

4. **Legal compliance moved from promise to product surface**  
   Bosco now exposes privacy and terms pages in both public and authenticated surfaces, and RGPD erasure is represented by an actual product flow rather than only legal copy.

5. **Roadmap sequencing improved when a real constraint was discovered**  
   The Epic 6 split (Android-first / iOS-deferred) was a pragmatic decision that protected delivery momentum and allowed Epic 7 to start without waiting on unavailable hardware.

---

## Challenges

1. **Story status drift remained unresolved**  
   Epic closure is recorded in `sprint-status.yaml`, but story files for 5.2, 5.3, and 5.4 do not reflect the same reality. This weakens trust in the artifacts at the exact moment they are supposed to support review and retrospective work.

2. **Manual operational follow-ups were mixed into "done" stories without a consistent closure mechanism**  
   Story 5.1 still called out missing Gmail auth-results capture, Story 5.3 included manual Sentry/Uptime dashboard setup, and Story 5.4 explicitly noted missing publication metadata at one stage. The team delivered the code, but the operational completion signal stayed fuzzy.

3. **Story 5.5 exposed an important RLS blind spot**  
   The first implementation attempted to set `profiles.disabled_at` using the authenticated user client, but the RLS policy from Story 5.2 intentionally forbids that field mutation. This caused the delete-account flow to fail until the operation moved behind the admin client.

4. **Production diagnostics were initially too generic for privileged flows**  
   "Failed to delete your account" was accurate but not operationally useful. The actual causes were harder to isolate than they needed to be until the flow began surfacing the real backend error message.

5. **Environment and domain changes created state-related confusion in real usage**  
   The installed app still pointed at the old domain until reinstalled, which looked like an auth regression even though the underlying flow was working. Production changes need a clearer smoke-check discipline around app/domain state.

---

## Key Insights

1. **RLS-sensitive stories need explicit write-path validation, not just schema correctness**  
   Story 5.2 correctly blocked direct user edits to `disabled_at`. Story 5.5 then failed because the implementation assumed ordinary authenticated writes would still work. Future stories that add guarded fields must include a "who is allowed to mutate this field?" review checkpoint.

2. **Operational work needs a different definition of done than pure application code**  
   DNS, dashboard alerting, SMTP, store metadata, domain changes, and deployed env vars are not side notes. They need explicit completion evidence or they become invisible residual risk.

3. **Artifact consistency matters most on infrastructure/legal epics**  
   When work is operationally complex, stale statuses and incomplete close-out notes are more damaging than usual because they obscure what was actually verified.

4. **Privileged and destructive flows deserve immediate post-deploy smoke tests**  
   Magic-link auth, account deletion, env-var wiring, and legal/public route reachability are all low-volume but high-impact flows. They should be checked immediately after deployment, not only inferred from local tests.

## Action Items

| # | Owner | Action | Success Criteria |
|---|-------|--------|------------------|
| 1 | Project Lead | **Choose one source of truth for story status** | `sprint-status.yaml` or story file status is declared authoritative, and the non-authoritative copy is either removed or treated as informational only |
| 2 | Project Lead | **Add an operational close-out checklist for infrastructure/legal stories** | Stories that touch DNS, SMTP, dashboard alerts, env vars, or publication metadata include an explicit checklist and evidence section before `done` |
| 3 | Dev | **Add an RLS mutation review checkpoint to story implementation** | Any story that introduces protected columns or privileged mutations explicitly documents who can write them and how that is tested |
| 4 | QA / Project Lead | **Create a post-deploy smoke checklist for high-risk flows** | Checklist includes magic-link auth, legal page reachability, delete-account flow, and env-dependent server actions |
| 5 | Dev | **Keep surfacing real backend error messages for destructive/admin actions** | User-visible failures on privileged flows expose actionable messages rather than only generic toasts |

## Epic 7 Preparation

### Context
Epic 7 is **Visual Storytelling - Photos, OG & Sharing**:
- Photo markers on the map
- Photo clustering
- Lightbox viewer
- Dynamic OG image generation
- Native/Web share button
- Dual CTA on public pages

### What Epic 5 unlocked for Epic 7
- **Monitoring foundation:** Epic 5.3 gives Epic 7 a place to observe OG/share/public-route failures in production
- **Legal baseline:** Epic 5.4 reduces compliance ambiguity as Bosco expands public storytelling and sharing surfaces
- **Account governance:** Epic 5.5 reduces RGPD exposure before photo-rich public features expand
- **Roadmap clarity:** the Epic 6 split removed the iPhone blocker from the immediate critical path, so Epic 7 can proceed as planned

### Risks for Epic 7
- **Visual QA risk:** Epic 7 is highly UI-heavy and will punish weak visual verification more than Epic 5 did
- **Platform verification risk:** OG images and share flows require real post-deploy validation, not only unit coverage
- **State complexity risk:** public/private surfaces, public pages, and mobile/share behavior will multiply the number of production-only edge cases

### Preparation Needed
- Finalize the story-status source-of-truth decision before Epic 7 accumulates more drift
- Carry a lightweight visual QA checklist into every Epic 7 story
- Define a minimal smoke test for share + public voyage preview behavior after deploy
- Reuse observability/logging conventions from Epic 5 instead of creating new ad hoc patterns
